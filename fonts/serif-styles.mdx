---
title: "Serif and mathematical Unicode font styles in Fontcy"
description: "Explore Fontcy's nine math Unicode styles — Bold Sans, Italic Sans, Monospace, Script, Fraktur, Double Struck, and more — with examples and use cases."
---

Fontcy's serif and math styles pull characters from Unicode's Mathematical Alphanumeric Symbols block (U+1D400–U+1D7FF). This block was originally designed for mathematical notation, but its distinctive letterforms make it popular for social media bios, usernames, and styled posts. All nine styles cover the full a–z and A–Z range.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Bold Sans">
    Bold Sans uses the Mathematical sans-serif bold block. Each letter is a heavier, sans-serif version of its regular counterpart — familiar but visually prominent.

    **Example:** 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗤𝘂𝗶𝗰𝗸 𝗕𝗿𝗼𝘄𝗻 𝗙𝗼𝘅

    **Unicode range:** U+1D5D4–U+1D607 (uppercase), U+1D608–U+1D63B (lowercase)

    **Best for:** Emphasising words in social posts, making a username stand out, or adding weight to a headline without switching to a decorative style.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Italic Sans">
    Italic Sans uses the Mathematical sans-serif italic block. Letters are slanted, clean, and easy to read — a lighter alternative to Bold Sans.

    **Example:** 𝘛𝘩𝘦 𝘘𝘶𝘪𝘤𝘬 𝘉𝘳𝘰𝘸𝘯 𝘍𝘰𝘹

    **Unicode range:** U+1D608–U+1D63B (uppercase), U+1D622–U+1D655 (lowercase)

    **Best for:** Titles, headers, and display names where you want a polished look without the heaviness of bold.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Bold Italic Sans">
    Bold Italic Sans combines the weight of Bold Sans with the slant of Italic Sans. It is the most visually forceful of the three sans-serif variants.

    **Example:** 𝙏𝙝𝙚 𝙌𝙪𝙞𝙘𝙠 𝘽𝙧𝙤𝙬𝙣 𝙁𝙤𝙭

    **Unicode range:** U+1D63C–U+1D66F (uppercase and lowercase)

    **Best for:** Strong emphasis, calls to action, or any text where you want maximum visual weight in a clean sans-serif form.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Monospace">
    Monospace uses the Mathematical monospace block. Every character occupies the same fixed width, giving text a typewriter or terminal aesthetic.

    **Example:** 𝚃𝚑𝚎 𝚀𝚞𝚒𝚌𝚔 𝙱𝚛𝚘𝚠𝚗 𝙵𝚘𝚡

    **Unicode range:** U+1D670–U+1D6A3

    **Best for:** Code-aesthetic content, tech usernames, or any context where you want a retro terminal look without using an actual code block.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Script">
    Script uses the Mathematical script block — an elegant, flowing cursive style. Uppercase letters are particularly ornate.

    **Example:** 𝒯𝒽ℯ 𝒬𝓊𝒾𝒸𝓀 ℬ𝓇ℴ𝓌𝓃 ℱℴ𝓍

    **Unicode range:** U+1D49C–U+1D4CF (with several characters drawn from Letterlike Symbols, U+2100–U+214F)

    **Best for:** Social bios, names, and anywhere you want an artistic or handwritten feel. One of the most popular styles on social platforms.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Bold Script">
    Bold Script is the heavier version of Script. The letterforms remain cursive and flowing, but the increased stroke weight makes them more legible at small sizes.

    **Example:** 𝓣𝓱𝓮 𝓠𝓾𝓲𝓬𝓴 𝓑𝓻𝓸𝔀𝓷 𝓕𝓸𝔁

    **Unicode range:** U+1D4D0–U+1D503

    **Best for:** Decorative headings, name styling, and any use case where Script looks a little too light for the context.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Fraktur">
    Fraktur replicates the blackletter calligraphy style common in medieval manuscripts and early German printing. It is distinctive but can be harder to read at a glance.

    **Example:** 𝔗𝔥𝔢 𝔔𝔲𝔦𝔠𝔨 𝔅𝔯𝔬𝔴𝔫 𝔉𝔬𝔵

    **Unicode range:** U+1D504–U+1D537 (with some characters from Letterlike Symbols)

    **Best for:** Gothic, medieval, or heavy-metal aesthetics. Popular for stylised band names, usernames, and artistic bios.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Bold Fraktur">
    Bold Fraktur thickens the Fraktur strokes for a stronger, darker look. The increased weight also makes individual characters slightly easier to distinguish.

    **Example:** 𝖙𝖍𝖊 𝖖𝖚𝖎𝖈𝖐 𝕭𝖗𝖔𝖜𝖓 𝕱𝖔𝖝

    **Unicode range:** U+1D56C–U+1D59F

    **Best for:** Situations where standard Fraktur feels too delicate — heavier gothic headings, band names, and dark-themed profiles.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Double Struck">
    Double Struck (also called blackboard bold) draws each letter with a doubled stroke, giving it a hollow or outlined appearance. It is widely used in mathematics to denote number sets such as ℝ (real numbers) and ℤ (integers).

    **Example:** 𝕋𝕙𝕖 𝕼𝕦𝕚𝕔𝕜 𝔹𝕣𝕠𝕨𝕟 𝔽𝕠𝕩

    **Unicode range:** U+1D538–U+1D56B (with several characters from Letterlike Symbols)

    **Best for:** Academic or mathematical aesthetics, science usernames, and any context where a clean outlined look fits.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
